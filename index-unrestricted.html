Candidacy Classes - PreReadings

Every PSL Candidate is required to take biweekly candidacy classes to introduce them to the basics of Marxism and to the Party's positions on global, historic, and ongoing struggles.

Unit 1: Socialism is Possible

The objective of this class is to explore the role of the party as an organizational and political instrument of the working class and to engage in discussion and deepen our collective understanding of the importance of building a revolutionary party in our context.

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. What led you to join the party?
  2. Why is a cadre type of organization necessary to succeed in revolutionary struggle?
  3. Why build a party instead of another type of organization?

01 - HISTORY OF THE PSL — PRE-CLASS MATERIALS Readings








The objective of this class is to cultivate a shared, concrete understanding of what socialism is, why we need it, and the fact that it’s entirely possible to build it in the U.S.. Through this class, we will understand socialism as the only solution to the today’s capitalist crises and why the a revolutionary party is necessary to achieve it, while also engaging in discussion about how we can use the ideas from Socialist Reconstruction to develop revolutionary consciousness and inspire the working class to envision a better future for everyone.

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. Reflecting on your answer in the first class about why you joined the party, what ideas led you to identify socialism as the answer?
  2. What were some misconceptions about socialism that were the biggest for you to overcome before you joined the party?
  3. When it comes to building socialist consciousness and preparing for a revolutionary situation, what things are in our control and what things aren't?

02 - SOCIALIST RECONSTRUCTION — PRE-CLASS MATERIALS Readings







The objective of this class is to understand the stakes of the political situation, our national orientation and the current tasks associated with our socialist intervention while also more deeply understanding what it means to be a member of the party--particularly the primary tasks of party building and cadre development in the current period.

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. Why is it important for the working class to engage in global politics? How has this built class consciousness and class organization?
  2. What does it take to recruit the best leaders of our class?

03 - CURRENT TASKS OF ALL MEMBERS — PRE-CLASS MATERIALS Readings













The objective of this class is to develop a basic understanding of how the contradictions inherent in capitalism create both subjective and objective foundations for a socialist revolution. This includes the ability to articulate fundamental concepts of Marxism—such as surplus value, exploitation, overproduction, and primitive accumulation—and to apply these concepts to personal experiences or political struggles. Additionally, it seeks to foster an understanding of Marxism as an essential tool, both as a theory and a method, for empowering working and oppressed peoples in advancing their side of the class struggle.

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. Why do we say that the capitalists and the working class have opposing interests? How does this relate to the struggle over value and surplus-value?
  2. What does it mean to think like a marxist?
  3. How is capitalism today, holding back humanity’s development or obstructing our productive power?
  4. How does class struggle drive history? How can marxism help you understand and lead struggles in your area?

04 - INTRO TO MARXISM — PRE-CLASS MATERIALS Readings




The objective of this class is to understand and express how Marxists use theory to formulate strategy and tactics in mass organizing and how they relate to party building, to understand the dialectical relationship between the Party and the masses along with how the Party has relates to both spontaneous uprisings and our own mass campaigns, and understand why mass organizing is necessary to build multinational proletarian unity and how to use Marxism to overcome the challenges and obstacles that come with it.

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. Why is the party's ideological intervention an important part of moving the masses? How does this distinguish us from other types of organizations?
  2. How can we build unity among such a massive group of people across different backgrounds and sectors of society?

Unit 2: Introduction to the Class Struggle

The objective of this class is to understand the class character of the American Revolution and the Constitution, explore the key elements in the formation of the United States and their relationship to the creation of the ideology of white supremacy, and to engage in discussion about the aims of the Civil War and the advances of Reconstruction.

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. What are some examples of ways that we see the U.S. political system shifting in order to preserve ruling class hegemony?
  2. What are some of the elements of the project of white supremacy that continue to be at work today?
  3. What lessons can we draw from the period of Reconstruction? What were some of the limitations to this and why was this period so short lived?

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. What does the history of multinational struggles tell us about the possibilities of building unity and solidarity to advance working class interests today?

Unit 3: Socialism in Power

The objective of this class is to deepen our understanding of the party’s view on the Soviet Union and the conditions before and after the revolution, to assess the real challenges and problems facing the governments that aspire to socialism, and to apply the historical materialist method to the history of the Russian Revolution and the Soviet Union.

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. Revolutions are rare events in human history. Rebellions, revolts, uprisings and coup events are ever-present throughout the history of class society. What made the Russian revolution of 1917 truly unique and different from all earlier revolutions?
  2. Why did Lenin call for the reorganization of the Russian economy in 1921 by introducing the New Economic Policy (NEP)? What was the essence of the NEP? Why did Lenin stress that the NEP was a retreat? What made the language of “retreat” important in Lenin’s public description of the NEP?








The objective of this class is to understand why the PSL defends the Soviet Union as a social system superior to capitalism while retaining an independent political assessment of any line or other aspect of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union, to popularly explain why and how the Soviet Union benefited its own people and the decolonization of the Global South, and to get a sense of the factors that led to the overthrow or collapse of the Soviet Union.

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. What are some of the key features that made the Soviet Union’s economy different from the major capitalist countries, such as its ability to avoid economic recessions and mass unemployment? What were some of the other benefits the working class people received from having a socialist economy?
  2. How do we characterize the Soviet Union? Socialist or a post-capitalist transitional government or both?
  3. What was Perestroika and Glasnost? Why did Perstroika lead to nationalist rivalries and conflicts within the Soviet Union in the second half of the 1980’s?
  4. What factors caused the Soviet working class to act as passive spectators?








The objective of this class is to have a basic understanding of why the PSL defends the China and the characteristics of the country today, deepen our understanding the impact of western imperialism on China as well as the origins and development of the new cold war, and highlight the distinctness of China’s revolutionary path.

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. How would you explain the “Century of Humiliation”?
  2. For what reasons did the Party decide to utilize capitalist methods of production and loosen the state’s monopoly on foreign trade? Why doesn’t this mean China has become capitalist or that it abandoned the path to socialism?
  3. How does China’s response to the 2008 economic crisis demonstrate the ongoing pursuit of socialism?
  4. What strategies is the U.S. pursuing in their attempt to overthrow the Chinese Communist Party and how do they relate to the history of modern China?










The objective of this class is to understand the history of the Cuban revolution as a continual struggle, first overthrowing its neo-colonialist government and now fighting U.S. imperialism while building socialism, deepen our understanding of how U.S. imperialism targets the revolution today and to be able to express its impact on Cuban society, highlight what Cuba’s socialist project entails and why only a socialist revolution has enabled Cuba to achieve and maintain their gains, and to engage in a discussion about why the party defends Cuba and what we do as a party and the larger mass movement to express solidarity.

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. What are your main takeaways from the Cuban Revolution? What example do the achievements of the Cuban revolution set for poor and working people in the United States?
  2. How would you explain to a friend how that process of consensus has been used in Cuba?
  3. Why does the progressive movement call the U.S. economic policy a “blockade” and not an “embargo”? What does the blockade entail?
  4. Why is the U.S. so intent on overthrowing Cuba’s working class government and why do we defend the Cuban Revolution?








Unit 4: The Struggle of Socialism & Liberation

The objective of this class is to understand the Party’s analysis of the roots of national oppression, how it operates in the United States today and how socialist governments have worked to eliminate it, to examine the struggles against racism through the lens of a national liberation and detonators of broader class struggle, and to engage in a discussion about what it means to build multi-national unity in a multi-national party and our task in the struggle for the liberation and equality of all nations.

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. Why is the National Question important today?
  2. How can we apply the historical lessons on the national question to international movements and political developments in other countries?
  3. How can we convince workers that overturning class society and building socialism is the key to national liberation without minimizing race or undermining their experiences with national oppression?










The objective of this class is to understand the Party’s analysis of the origins of women’s oppression, how it manifests in society and why only socialism will lead to women’s liberation, examine the struggle for reproductive rights and how the attack on abortion access is a key issue within the women’s right movements that impacts the entire working class, and engage in a discussion on what it means to fight for women’s liberation and the critical importance of developing women cadre and leaders within the Party.

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. How does the ruling class indoctrinate us from a young age about the role of women in society? How does this impact oppression?
  2. What does the modern abortion rights struggle and ballot success of protections for reproductive rights tell us about the potential to unite the women’s movement across ideological divides?










The objective of this class is to understand the Party’s analysis of the origins of LGBTQ oppression, how it manifests in society and why only socialism will lead to LGBTQ liberation, examine the LGBTQ community’s historical struggle against violent bigotry and oppression, their pursuit for equal rights and their fight against today’s attacks, and to engage in a discussion on what it means to fight for LGBTQ liberation and the importance of everyone in the party fighting for LGBTQ rights

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. From a materialist standpoint, what are some connections between women's oppression and the oppression of LGBTQ people?
  2. How can we combat cooptation and misleadership by liberal forces within the LGBTQ movement?
  3. What are some ways we can make innroads within the LGBTQ community and recruit from that section of the population?
  4. Outside of pride month, what is our role as organizers both within the LGBTQ community and outside of it in supporting LGBTQ struggles? What are some creative ways that we can fight for LGBTQ liberation now in the future?

  2. What is a union? What is the role of unions in a capitalist society from a communist perspective?

  5. Why do we engage in the labor struggle as Party members?








Unit 5: Becoming a Full Member

The objective of this class is to define democratic centralism and understand how we apply it as a guiding organizational principle to our work in the party, examine how the PSL builds internal democracy and how our party’s structures have helped further our organization’s capacity and political goals, and to understand the PSL’s approach to leadership and cadre development.

Keep these guiding questions in mind as you read:
  1. How have you observed democratic centralism in practice locally and/or nationally? What might be some repercussions for our organization if democratic centralism is not adhered to?
  2. What are some ways that the unit structure has allowed the party to expand its reach, widen its visibility and/or develop more leaders?
  3. What role can we play as individuals in strengthening the party's collective unity?
